# Finding Lifecycle

This document defines the complete lifecycle of a finding — from the moment the agent generates it to the moment it reaches a closed state. It covers all valid states, valid transitions between states, audit trail requirements, and special handling rules for reclassification findings and economic trade-off assessments.

The AI Architect should read this document before designing the finding data model and the disposition tracking system.

---

## What a finding is

A finding is a structured output generated by the agent when it detects a gap, inconsistency, or compliance risk in a controlled document. Every finding is:

- **Bound to a source** — a specific document version, a specific requirement identifier, and a specific analysis timestamp
- **Cited** — every finding references the standard clause or regulatory requirement that makes the detected gap significant
- **Classified** — every finding carries the IMDRF category and software item safety class that determined its severity
- **Immutable** — the content of a finding cannot be edited after generation. A reviewer who disagrees with a finding records their disagreement in the disposition rationale, not by modifying the finding.
- **Non-binding** — a finding is a proposal. It does not require any specific action. The human reviewer decides what to do.

## State definitions

**Draft** — the finding has been generated by the analysis engine but has not yet been delivered to the assigned owner. This state is transient — the agent moves findings from Draft to Sent automatically upon successful delivery. If delivery fails, the finding remains in Draft and the delivery is retried. A finding that cannot be delivered within the configured timeout is escalated to the system administrator.

**Sent** — the finding has been delivered to the assigned owner's inbox (UI, notification, or integrated workflow system). The owner has not yet opened or acknowledged it. Findings remain in Sent until the owner opens the finding detail view, at which point they transition to In Review automatically.

**In Review** — the owner has opened the finding and is actively reviewing it. This is the primary working state. From In Review, the owner can select any of the five terminal dispositions (Accept, Reject, Escalate, Out of scope) or two intermediate dispositions (Clarify, Route).

**Clarify** — the owner needs more information before making a disposition decision. The owner submits a question or clarification request. The finding returns to In Review when the requested information is provided. There is no limit on the number of Clarify cycles, but each cycle is logged.

**Route** — the owner determines that the finding belongs to a different role or person. They specify the target owner and the finding transitions back to Sent for the new owner. The routing history is logged. The original owner is notified when the re-assigned owner makes a final disposition.

**Defer** — the owner acknowledges the finding is valid but cannot act on it immediately. Requires a deferral reason and a target review date. The finding returns to Sent automatically when the target review date is reached, and the next reviewer sees it with the deferral history. Defer is not a terminal state — a deferred finding must eventually reach Accept, Reject, Escalate, or Out of scope. There is no limit on the number of Defer cycles, but each cycle is logged and the cumulative deferral duration is visible in the finding detail view.

**Accept** — the finding is valid and the owner commits to taking action. Requires a rationale of at least one sentence. The finding transitions to Resolved when the owner confirms that the required action has been completed (for example, an SRS update has been committed and the traceability link is in place).

**Reject** — the finding is not applicable in this context. Requires a mandatory rationale explaining why the finding does not apply. The finding transitions directly to Closed. A rejected finding can be re-opened only by a system administrator if new information emerges.

**Escalate** — the finding requires attention above the normal review level. Requires an escalation reason. Automatically notifies the configured escalation target (typically the RA/PRRC role). The finding transitions to Resolved when the escalation has been handled and the handling outcome is documented.

**Out of scope** — the finding references something outside the current review boundary. Requires a brief explanation. The finding transitions to Closed. This disposition should be used sparingly — if a finding is consistently out of scope, the agent's routing configuration should be updated to prevent similar findings from being delivered to the same owner.

**Resolved** — the finding has been accepted and the required action has been completed, or an escalation has been handled. This is a terminal state. A resolved finding contributes to the agent's traceability evidence — it records what was found, what action was taken, and when.

**Closed** — the finding has been rejected or marked out of scope. This is a terminal state. A closed finding is retained in the audit log with its rationale.

## Audit trail requirements

Every state transition is logged with:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Finding ID | Unique, immutable identifier assigned at generation |
| Timestamp | UTC timestamp of the transition |
| From state | The state before the transition |
| To state | The state after the transition |
| Actor | Identity of the user or system that triggered the transition |
| Rationale | Required for Accept, Reject, Escalate, Out of scope, Route, Defer — optional for others |

The audit log is append-only. No entry can be modified or deleted after it is written. This is the primary regulatory evidence artefact for demonstrating that the agent's findings were systematically reviewed and dispositioned.

The audit log must be exportable as a structured file (JSON or CSV minimum) for import into the QMS or for direct review by regulatory authority personnel during inspections. The export must preserve all fields above and must include the finding content (source document, clause citation, detected gap) alongside the lifecycle events.

## Special handling — reclassification findings

A reclassification finding is generated when the agent detects that a SRS change may affect the IEC 62304 safety class of a software item or the IMDRF significance category of the device. These findings require a mandatory acknowledgement step before any other disposition is available.

**Why mandatory acknowledgement:** A missed reclassification is the highest-consequence failure mode the agent can produce. If an item is reclassified upward and the team does not act on it, the subsequent verification evidence will be insufficient for the new safety class. Discovered during a submission review, this requires the entire verification cycle to be repeated. The mandatory acknowledgement ensures the finding cannot be dismissed without a deliberate, documented decision.

**Mandatory acknowledgement flow:**

1. The finding is delivered with a reclassification flag prominently displayed.
2. Before the standard disposition options are available, the owner must confirm one of two statements:
   - "I confirm this reclassification risk is valid and will initiate a formal reassessment." — This automatically generates an Escalate disposition to the RA/PRRC role and the Software Architect.
   - "I confirm this reclassification risk does not apply because: [rationale]." — This records the rejection of the reclassification flag and makes the standard disposition options available.
3. Either outcome is logged as part of the finding's audit trail.

A reclassification flag cannot be dismissed without a documented rationale. There is no option to simply close the finding without addressing the reclassification question.

## Special handling — economic trade-off assessments

Some findings include an economic trade-off assessment — a structured summary of the full regulatory cost of implementing the proposed SRS change, enabling the team to make an informed decision about whether the change should proceed as specified.

A trade-off assessment is not a severity level and does not change the disposition options available. It is an additional section within the finding that the reviewer can use to inform their disposition.

A trade-off assessment is generated when:
- The agent detects that a SRS change would trigger multiple high-severity findings across different document areas simultaneously
- The agent detects that a SRS change may push the device to a higher IMDRF category
- The agent detects that a SRS change may push an AI/ML modification outside PCCP boundaries

The trade-off assessment presents:
- The regulatory obligations triggered by the change (which documents must be updated, which submissions may be required)
- An estimated effort range for fulfilling those obligations
- An alternative scope option if one is identifiable (for example, a narrower version of the change that stays within existing approval boundaries)

The reviewer uses this information to decide whether to proceed with the change as specified, modify the scope, or defer the change. The disposition records which decision was made.

## Finding retention

Findings are retained permanently in the audit log. They are never deleted, even after a device is retired from the market. This is consistent with the technical file and DHF retention requirements under both EU MDR (typically 10 years for most devices, 15 years for implantable devices) and FDA (21 CFR 820, typically 2 years beyond expected device life).

The agent's analysis store (the non-authoritative working store) may have data TTL policies for performance reasons. The audit log is separate from the analysis store and is not subject to TTL policies.

## Finding identifiers

Every finding is assigned a unique identifier at generation time. The identifier format is:

```
F-{YYYYMMDD}-{SEQUENCE}
```

Example: `F-20260415-0047` — the 47th finding generated on 15 April 2026.

The identifier is immutable. If a finding is re-generated after a SRS change (for example, a previous finding was resolved and the same gap reappears after a subsequent change), the new finding receives a new identifier. The link between the new finding and the previous finding that addressed the same gap is recorded in the finding metadata but the identifiers remain distinct.

SRS requirement identifiers, risk control identifiers, and software item identifiers referenced in a finding are those that were current at the time of finding generation and are bound to the specific document version that was analysed. If identifiers change in a subsequent document version, the finding retains the identifiers from the analysed version — it does not auto-update.
